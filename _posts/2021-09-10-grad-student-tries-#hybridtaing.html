This semester so far has been a whirlwind, and we just finished Week 4.

Currently, I’m a teaching assistant for an in-person senior-level course with a semester-long group project. I run the lab sections where the students get into their groups and work on their project as well as do group check-ins (AKA scrums) with me each week. Due to the pandemic, I’ve also had to adapt to being a hybrid lab instructor. For the class, there is a videoconferencing session that’s set up for students who are quarantined to utilize.

Hybrid courses sound like the best of both worlds, right?

Unfortunately, if you agree with that statement, we currently don’t see eye-to-eye. Hybrid courses force instructors to split their attention across multiple modalities, the students in the classroom – who may be asking questions and chatting to the instructor - and the students in the video call – who might be using the chat feature, raising their hands, etc. to get the instructor’s attention.

Inclusion and accessibility are two things that I am currently learning about in my Engineering and Science Education courses. Although I believe down to my core that all courses should be accessible to all students, hybrid courses are not something I wish to have to teach again. However, I do think there are some positive aspects to holding hybrid courses:

Pros of Hybrid courses:

  • For students:
    • Convenient
    • Stops the spread of germs
    • Can mute camera/mic
    • Can be multitasking/not paying attention
  • For professors:
    • Stops the spread of germs
    • Allows students to stay on track in the course if they must miss
    • Automatic nametags on faces

I think that with some proper finagling, hybrid courses could work, but in the uncertainty of a pandemic, I think that it just makes a professor’s job more difficult than it needs to be. For example, in my classroom there is a semester long project that these teams are working on each day in lab. Some students must attend lab via videoconference due to their status as a quarantined individual. Others find it more convenient to attend online due to some outside factors in their lives. This brings extra stress to me, the TA, because I always need to have the videoconference up and running each class. It also brings extra stress on the group members who chose to attend in-person, because now they must communicate to everyone in the classroom but also with their teammates who are online. I hear a lot of students having to repeat themselves for their online teammates. This leads into my perceived cons of hybrid courses:

Cons of Hybrid courses:

  • For students:
    • Teamwork breakout sessions are difficult to contribute w/ in person students
    • Online courses are harder to pay attention to overall
  • For professors:
    • Ensuring all students are heard/attended to
    • Not knowing who will be in-person or online on any given day

However, some of these cons depend on the actual students within the classroom. I’m sure there are some classrooms and professors where a hybrid style works very well for them. I’m unsure if it’s me and my inexperience with being in a leadership role within a classroom, or if hybrid courses are just difficult to do correctly, but my experiences with hybrid classes and summer camps have been pretty tough experiences.

I'm not saying that it's all bad when it comes to TA-ing. Just the other day, two of my students saw me walking to our class and asked if they could join me. We chit-chatted, they asked me a lot of questions, and I went to class knowing a little bit more about them. I'm glad that after a year and some change of being online, I'm able to connect with some of my students and learn about their lives outside of my classroom.
